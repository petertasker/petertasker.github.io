title: "Red Star Over Glasgow"

The sky is grey and unmoving in Glasgow city centre. A light and de-industrial air holds its ground, disallowing a full breath in, finding itself inappropriate for both heavy and light clothing. I find myself on the great thoroughfare of Glasgow city: Buchanan Street. Behind me stands a subway station which stands in lieu of what was once a great Victorian work of locomotive architecture. All that stands there now is concrete, bollards, and men dressed in black clothes with black balaclavas with black bikes. At the cross of Argyll Street and Buchanan Street, near what remains of the Victorian railway hub, are orange cones and grey steel fences, barring passers-by from entering the area it encloses. A smiling old man with dolls sits facing the entrance of the nearby shopping centre playing doo-wops out his speaker, expecting payment in return. Looking up the street---a half mile of commerce, evangelism, and money laundering---people walk listlessly to and from their destinations. No one stops and takes in the view on Buchanan Street---there is no view. 

Saturday seems to be the day where people set their stalls. Looking over the heads of the passers by---families, young men and women, pensioners---three stands are visible: two communist and one Palestinian. I know that behind the pro-Palestine tent is a pro-israeli tent, and I am aware of tents further up the street. I walk up to the first communist tent which is red and has the words "SOCIALIST WORKERS PARTY". I introduce myself and state my intentions. The young comrade, with a heavy dose of skepticism agrees to be spoken to. I imagine standing under a tent that says "SOCIALIST WORKERS PARTY" attracts a lot of public agitation, and I do my best to put him at ease whilst sprinkling in some Marxist jargon.

"This is our regular weekly campaign stall. We see the vast waves of crisis the world is facing. We've just lost a prime Minister, we've seen hottest recorded week in June. In these times it's very important to spread revolutionary consciousness. We believe solving these crises the system must be torn down---the system *cannot* be reformed from within.

"This year, we are focused on building Marxism Festival---our yearly event in London, the largest space for the left to discuss not only the current crises but some historical theoretical concepts too.

I refer the young *tovarishch* to the other Red stall with words on it which read "COMMUNIST PARTY".

"It's not the same party... The Communist Party views the struggle as through a more Marxist-Leninist-Stalinist lens." I await his name. It's called the SWP after all... "We take more of a Trotskyite perspective. But *when push comes to shove* we will collaborate. For example, a band of *fascists* were grouped up [the top of Buchanan Street], and it was very much a case of 'If you get in trouble, call us'"

"...There are much more productive things to do than to argue with [The Communist Party]. *Friends of Israel* are within eyeshot. We wouldn't agitate. We prefer keeping to ourselves.
    
"... We get some passers-by shouting 'Scum! Scum!', but plenty of people, especially those who are destitute with real concerns, and we hope we can answer them. And we won't always reach amicable agreement but they still make for productive conversations, and we can still sling them a paper. The people who actually engage with us tend to be more sympathetic.

"What have I been reading? Well, I am quite new in the Party. I joined about seven months ago, so I'm reading into the Marxist tradition. I just started on *The Revolutionary Ideas of Karl Marx* by Alex Callinicos. 

"The point is to wrestle Marx away from the people who use him to their own ends."

"i.e. Stalinism...", says I. 

"Yes, and anyone of his tendency and beyond."

"... We see Russia-Ukraine as an inter-imperialist war. Russia is undergoing imperialist expansion, but so too [has] NATO push[ed] eastwards, and it has coalesced into a meat-grinder. You often forget, but its horrific, whats going on on the front-line. And all the while, the interest invested by the war-hawks means that it will never end.

I shake the young mans hand and give him a couple of pounds for his cause. I head for the second Red tent.  Ruminating on my conversation with the young new member, I remark on how name-droppish the conversation is, and how much of a time capsule I felt I was in. I arrive at the Communist Party tent, state my  name, age, and occupation, and ask if anyone would be able to be interviewed. 

Keith Stoddart, a man with a wikipedia entry, accepts my challenge. We walk into "his office": a blue doorframe with an overhang. The man of 69 with a friendly disposition does not have any reservations on being put to questions. "... I'm the chair of the Scottish Committee of the Communist Party of Britain."

"...The first communist stall in Glasgow happened in 1920 outside what is now the Gallery of Modern Art. We used to call it 'Speaker's Corner.' there'd by a variety of souls and proselytise. This went on until the 50s, maybe the 60s! Saturday was here (at G.O.M.A) and Sunday would be in the Barras (a market to the east of the city centre, looking onto the Clyde).

"... Our newspaper, *The Morning Star* comes out six days a week, (!). It's 94 years old. It's an alternative to all the other papers on the news stands. Its unequivocally on the side of those engaged in struggles, be it at home, industrially, or internationally.

"... It used to be called The Daily Worker until 1966. There were two Chartist papers: *The Northern Star* and *The Morning Courier*

"We've always been engaged in public work. One time we would have had stalls all over Glasgow, and we would have sold literature... We would talk to people and tell them about why we believe what we do and why we think life would be better. 

"To put it simply, as Communists, we believe that this planet has the resources to give everybody a decent life. Instead, one percent have absolutely wonderful lives, if you believe social media. We in the West get by. We have poverty, but we don't have absolute poverty. A lot of our prosperity at the expense of those in the Global South.

"Children in Bangladesh are working for a quid a day to make T-shirts that we then buy for £1 in Primark. These things are seen as so negligible that we just throw them in the bin, and the people who are making them are seen as negligible and can get laid off in an instant. We see everything is done the wrong way, and our we believe our way would be much more equitable for everybody.

"... We normally get told to go back to Russia. Russia hasn't been socialist since 1991, so I don't know why we'd go to one of the most developed (!) capitalist countries in the world. We don't copy China, but we do take inspiration that by 1949 they've been able to take more people out of absolute poverty than any other time in history. 

We continue talking about China. While he lectures me, I wonder where these people buy these tents. They seem to be uniformly similar. Is there a regulation standard to these tents? Do I need a permit if I want to set up a tent? I return to the mans arguments, and inwardly cringe  as he talks about the success of Chinese and Russian industrialisation. I keep a poker-face and he directs the topic of conversation to Scottish industrialism and immigration.
