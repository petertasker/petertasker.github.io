title: "Red Star Over Glasgow"
permalink: /writing/red-star-over-glasgow/
---

The sky is grey and unmoving in Glasgow city centre. A light, de-industrial air holds its ground, disallowing a full breath in, finding itself inappropriate for both heavy and light clothing. I find myself on the great thoroughfare of Glasgow city: Buchanan Street. Behind me stands a subway station which stands in lieu of what was once a great Victorian work of locomotive architecture. All that stands there now is concrete, bollards, and men dressed in black clothes with black balaclavas and black bikes. At the cross of Argyll Street and Buchanan Street, near what remains of the Victorian railway hub, are orange cones and grey steel fences, barring passers-by from entering the area they enclose. A smiling old man with dolls sits facing the entrance of the nearby shopping centre, playing doo-wops out of his speaker, expecting payment in return. Looking up the street — a half-mile of commerce, evangelism, and money laundering — people walk listlessly to and from their destinations. No one stops and takes in the view on Buchanan Street — there is no view.

Saturday seems to be the day where people set their stalls. Looking over the heads of the passers-by — families, young men and women, pensioners — three stands are visible: two communist and one Palestinian. I know that behind the pro-Palestine tent is a pro-Israeli tent, and I am aware of tents further up the street. I walk up to the first communist tent, which is red and has the words "SOCIALIST WORKERS PARTY". It has two very young-looking people, no younger than 20. I introduce myself and state my intentions. The young comrade, with a heavy dose of scepticism, agrees to be interviewed. I imagine standing under a tent that says "SOCIALIST WORKERS PARTY" attracts a lot of public agitation, and I do my best to put him at ease whilst sprinkling in some Marxist jargon.

"This is our regular weekly campaign stall. We see the vast waves of crisis the world is facing. We've just lost a Prime Minister; we've seen the hottest recorded week in June. In these times it's very important to spread revolutionary consciousness. We believe that to solve these crises the system must be torn down — the system *cannot* be reformed from within.

"This year, we are focused on building the Marxism Festival — our yearly event in London, the largest space for the left to discuss not only the current crises but some historical theoretical concepts too."

I refer the young *tovarishch* to the other red stall, with words on it which read "COMMUNIST PARTY".

"It's not the same party... The Communist Party views the struggle through a more Marxist-Leninist-Stalinist lens." I await his name. It's called the SWP, after all... "We take more of a Trotskyite perspective. But *when push comes to shove* we will collaborate. For example, a band of *fascists* were grouped up at the top of Buchanan Street, and it was very much a case of 'If you get in trouble, call us.'"

"...There are much more productive things to do than to argue with the Communist Party. *Friends of Israel* are within eyeshot. We wouldn't agitate. We prefer keeping to ourselves."

"...We get some passers-by shouting 'Scum! Scum!' But plenty of people, especially those who are destitute with real concerns, speak to us, and we hope we can answer them. We won't always reach amicable agreement, but they still make for productive conversations, and we can still sling them a paper. The people who actually engage with us tend to be more sympathetic.

"What have I been reading? Well, I am quite new to the Party. I joined about seven months ago, so I'm reading into the Marxist tradition. I just started on *The Revolutionary Ideas of Karl Marx* by Alex Callinicos.

"The point is to wrestle Marx away from the people who use him to their own ends."

"i.e. Stalinism...", says I.

"Yes, and anyone of his tendency and beyond."

"...We see Russia-Ukraine as an inter-imperialist war. Russia is undergoing imperialist expansion, but so too has NATO pushed eastwards, and it has coalesced into a meat-grinder. You often forget, but it's horrific, what's going on on the front line. And all the while, the interest invested by the war-hawks means that it will never end."

I shake the young man's hand and give him a couple of pounds for his cause, and get handed a leaflet (they called it *literature*) advertising "MARXISM FESTIVAL". This is the only quantifiable bit of work the representative of the SWP gives me showing what they are actually doing. I don't blame the young gentleman for failing to say what the SWP *actually does* in Glasgow; he has been put out high and dry. Passionate: yes; experienced: no.

I head for the second red tent. Ruminating on my conversation with the young new member, I remark further on how name-droppish the conversation is, and how much of a time capsule I felt I was in. They're not quite *cosplaying* per se, but I didn't get the feeling they were spending their Saturday productively. I arrive at the Communist Party tent, state my name, age, and occupation, and ask if anyone would be able to be interviewed.

Keith Stoddart, a man with a Wikipedia entry, accepts my challenge. We walk into "his office": a blue doorframe with an overhang. The man of 69, with a friendly disposition, does not have any reservations about being put to questions. "...I'm the chair of the Scottish Committee of the Communist Party of Britain."

"...The first communist stall in Glasgow happened in 1920 outside what is now the Gallery of Modern Art. We used to call it 'Speaker's Corner'. There'd be a variety of souls who would proselytise... This went on until the '50s, maybe the '60s! Saturday was here at GoMA, and Sunday would be in the Barras — a market to the east of the city centre, looking onto the Clyde.

"...Our newspaper, *The Morning Star*, comes out six days a week. It's 94 years old. It's an alternative to all the other papers on the news stands. It's unequivocally on the side of those engaged in struggles, be it at home, industrially, or internationally.

"...It used to be called The Daily Worker until 1966. There were two Chartist papers: *The Northern Star* and *The Morning Courier*.

"We've always been engaged in public work. At one time we would have had stalls all over Glasgow, and we would have sold literature... We would talk to people and tell them about why we believe what we do and why we think life would be better.

"To put it simply, as Communists, we believe that this planet has the resources to give everybody a decent life. Instead, one per cent have absolutely wonderful lives, if you believe social media. We in the West get by. We have poverty, but we don't have absolute poverty. A lot of our prosperity comes at the expense of those in the Global South.

"Children in Bangladesh are working for a quid a day to make T-shirts that we then buy for £1 in Primark. These things are seen as so negligible that we just throw them in the bin, and the people who are making them are seen as negligible and can get laid off in an instant. We see everything is done the wrong way, and we believe our way would be much more equitable for everybody.

"...We normally get told to go back to Russia. Russia hasn't been socialist since 1991, so I don't know why we'd go to one of the most developed capitalist countries in the world. We don't copy China, but we do take inspiration from the fact that since 1949 they've been able to take more people out of absolute poverty than at any other time in history.

"...25% of all cars sold in Britain year-to-date have been built in China. It's inspirational that it has taken them seventy years to do what took us 300!"

We continue talking about China. While he lectures me, I wonder where these people buy their tents. They seem to be uniformly similar. Is there a regulation standard for these tents? I return to the man's arguments and inwardly cringe as he talks about the success of Chinese and Russian industrialisation. I keep a poker face and he attempts to direct the topic of conversation to Scottish industrialism.

I can't help myself: "You are alluding to Mao here — a very violent and anti-democratic man. What is your party's method of getting into power? How will you achieve your aims of a socialist society?"

"Violent revolution does *not* work. Entering Parliament would be great. We are in the trade unions, too — that's where we have our strongest base. We believe you must highlight the issues in society and highlight the solutions."

While Mr. Stoddart, a very skilful and passionate orator, talks at great length about the problems Scotland, England, and Wales face — problems which in his view are caused by capitalism and solved by socialism — I can't get past the firestorm of seemingly contradictory statements that he has no issue reconciling: "We respect Mr. Mao for what he did to Chinese industrial relations." "We do not believe in violence." "We participate in democracy."
It strikes me that their revered state of Chinese industrialism was achieved through violence and anti-democratic oppression. The very fact that these comrades are allowed to set out their stalls is thanks to the liberal laws and traditions of the UK. If the means to your goal are against your principles as a party, then surely the outcome is subordinate.

I return to him: "...We *do* co-operate with the other socialist parties. Last night, I was at a meeting with the SWP, the Scottish Socialist Party — not to be confused with the Socialist Party of Scotland, who were also there. Also there were Labour Left people, Green Party members, and members from Your Party." I miss the obvious Monty Python reference.

"We work together in campaigns against anti-fascism, anti-racism. If members from the other parties are in trade unions, I sit in. I am a member of Unite the Union... We have a longer game than the other parties, and we believe that the people who will bring about change are the people who need it most. Other parties look at single issues, whether that be women or race, and while we accept that and also consider it, we look at these issues through a class lens. Take women, for example: we support the Women's Liberation movement, but we don't see it as 'women's issues' — we see women as being doubly oppressed; oppressed as working-class people and oppressed as women. The common factor, you can see, is class.

"But we are in maybe 85%–90% alignment with other parties on the left, especially in relation to international work. I have been to Venezuela a few times, including as an election observer, and it's very different there. They have been able to get rid of those nasty favelas. A quarter of a million houses have been built over there. It's a great achievement. The people are very proud of these houses; they paint them all sorts of colours and call them beautiful."

He continues talking about the electrification of Venezuelan houses and its benefits: "The women whom I was chatting with were thrilled; they would get four hours of their day back because they wouldn't have to gather wood and cook, all thanks to this little grill. These are very practical applications to increase living standards."

"This is where we see the battlefield: in trade unions, through community organisation, community groups. It's about raising consciousness." I am again impressed by Mr. Stoddart's passion and drive. This man clearly has experience and has given his life to his cause. I have been speaking to him for about 15 minutes, and not one word of his has felt superficial or less than wholehearted.

"...When I left school in the '60s, there were 26 shipyards in Scotland. How many are there now? Three. There is still a massive industrial base in Glasgow. We would support reindustrialisation in Glasgow. You need those skills. We have young people with skills who are working in pubs and in restaurants, and there's nothing wrong with those jobs, but they're not working to their full potential. Our young educated class are leaving for Australia while we in Britain are facing crisis after crisis. We must pay them what they are worth, otherwise they will continue to leave.

"...Our analysis on the Ukraine question is that the EU, and to an extent the USA, are using Ukraine to have a pop at Russia." I can tell here that the "analysis" is the "NATO enlargement" argument. I let him continue: "NATO agreed not to expand past East Germany. Since then, Turkey, Poland etc. have joined, and this expansion has been accelerated by capitalism. People in the Eastern Bloc are probably worse off than they were 30 years ago...

I interject: "Finland, Latvia, Estonia... All these countries were once in the Eastern Bloc, and they clearly don't want to be in the Russian sphere of influence. You saw Georgia attacked in 2008 by Russia, Crimea and the Donbas in 2014, and now this full invasion four years ago."

"I think there's a lot of Western interference. We are not here to defend Russia. But it's interesting. I was in Riga a few years ago. Latvia has brought in laws that only ethnic Latvians can get a passport, meaning ethnic Russians who moved there in the post-war era can do nothing..."

"...When the Baltic States broke away from the Soviet Union, there was an immediate power crisis because all their power was imported from Russia proper."

Returning to immigration, Mr. Stoddart says: "We have stolen talent from many countries because we cannot pay British people properly. My cardiologist is Polish, the doctor who performed my biopsy was Indian, and the trauma nurse was Nepalese. And these people get paid better than they would at home, but still not what they deserve.

"We believe that if you have earned a five-year degree, you should stay in Britain for five years. We would of course pay them the wage they deserve. They are leaving because of the poor wage, after all."

After being given two five-year plans, I ask Mr. Stoddart for his political hero: "James Connolly — and not because of 1916 Ireland. He is most famous as an Irish Nationalist and as a great advocate for socialism, but what I admire about Connolly is that he took Marxism and broke it into ten-minute conversations called *Workshop Talks*, where he'd break down capitalism. A thing designed for you to read while on your ten-minute tea break."

"...And I suppose Willie Gallacher, who was the MP for Paisley and the last Communist Party Member of Parliament."

I shake the man's hand and thank him for his time. I quickly google "James Connolly" and remark on his moustache. It is a very similar moustache to Mr. Stoddart's. I wonder if Mr. Stoddart has groomed himself to share the same characteristics as his socialist hero, or if a commanding moustache is a symptom of passionate socialism.

I return to the Communist Party tent and remark on the pin badges. There are three sections of this portable display. One is all things China. There are many copies of the proud Chinese mainland with its red and yellow flag overlaid. The next section is Bolshevik and especially Lenin-esque. I can understand why, but I feel that religious idolisation of people is very much against the original Bolshevik spirit of militant atheism. The final display is of various Soviet monuments, milestones, and events: the Government building of the Byelorussian SSR, now Belarus; a pin badge honouring the *Mir* space programme; and various other items which I employ Mr. Stoddart to translate for me. A man slips a red advertisement into my hands, and I ask a lady if I could buy a pin badge. I choose one from the Lenin display. Vladimir Ilyich's bald head is overlaid on a red star lit by the flame of revolution. It is captioned "Всегда Готов!" — "Always Prepared!" This amuses me greatly.

A group of East Asian tourists amass near the stall. I say to Mr. Stoddart: "Here's your congregation!" A working-class boy with a local accent behind the display asks where I'm from, and multiple faces leave and join the tent, socialising with each other, talking about some event they were at the night prior. I leave the gang to their business.

I make my way to my next item: the first Palestine tent I had seen from the bottom of the street. Three old-guard Glasgow guys are manning the station. They are selling wristbands, t-shirts, and flogging leaflets for their cause.

"Who are you with?"

"I'm not with anybody, Sir. I am interviewing all the stalls today and seeing what their cause is." This stall shares the SWP's caution towards citizen-journalist types.

"We're the Glasgow Palestine Human Rights Campaign. This began around the year 2002. I had always been very busy with my own life, looking after my children with my wife, to pay attention to the news. My wife woke up one morning and said: 'What's going on in Iraq is wrong. I'm going to be part of the *human shield*.' En route to Iraq, we stopped in Palestine, and ended up near one of the many refugee camps. Soldiers came in and killed a couple of kids at that point. There were three refugee camps in Bethlehem. Families take these children in — children whose parents have been killed. My wife was talking to one of the families and said: 'Our girl's dream is to become a nurse, but she can't.' Bethlehem has a great big wall around it. They don't allow people to walk freely. It's all checkpoints. But that doesn't take her dream away. It's always in her mind."

"...We came back and set up our campaign here in Glasgow. This area has turned into a sort of political hub. There's a great deal of energy here!

"The aim of our campaign is to fundraise for Palestine. All our merchandise proceedings go to lots of causes — disabled people, nurseries, theatre groups, operations... Since the 7th of October, we've focused our efforts mostly on Gaza as opposed to the broader region. We still from time to time donate to people outwith Gaza, but Gaza is where the money is most needed.

"...So far we've raised £120,000 right out of this stall. There's a lot of public sympathy. In the early days of our campaign, it was a bit more hostile."

Behind the gentleman is the *Friends of Israel* tent. "They arrived about eight years ago, so they must feel they're on the back foot! They deny the genocide. They deny the famine and what is going on in Palestine. They're pretty nasty people. They come down here and try to wind us up."

"We used to try to get people to visit Palestine to see what's really going on. The Israeli authorities don't like people to see these things. I got banned from Israel about five years ago. My wife got banged up in Tel Aviv, handcuffed for a couple of days, then put on a flight home and banned. She was in the second-last flotilla and again got treated very poorly."

I ask him about the current situation. "I see a lot less of Palestine on the news, and they've announced an end to the war. Is that true?"

"It's still happening, the troubles in Gaza. The brutality is the same. The only difference is the number of deaths."

A man catches the attention of my interviewee: "That man there is a Zionist lawyer. He got charged for setting up a fake Facebook account pretending to be a pro-Palestine protestor. He almost got struck off. Nasty guy."

Another man inside the tent interjects: "He used to turn up at my house!"

In an offhand remark, my new friend mentions Iran. "What's your position on Iran?" I ask.

"I am not for or against the government of Iran, but you have to look at it like this: America and Israel attacked Iran. You'd expect them to retaliate. America goes around bullying Venezuela and all these other countries, thinking they can get away with it. I think Iran has the right to ask for its own terms. Iran hasn't been to war with anybody — it's always been other countries that have instigated against them."

"Through proxies, they have," says I.

"We do that too, and so does America."

Other customers come up to the stall, and I feel my goodwill has expired. My pan-Arabist friend starts speaking to the other people, returns to me, and apologises for his curtness. I insist he has no need to apologise, and that he is doing me a service by giving me his time.

We return to Iran: "Iran has human rights issues, but so does everywhere else. It's hypocrisy. Look at the West Bank — there are two types of licence plates: one is green and one is yellow. Can you guess why there are two? I mentioned the checkpoints. They can't access their own land. In the West Bank, if you haven't lived on your land for three years, the Israeli state can take it. You can't say there are any human rights over there." A good point, well made.

I ask him finally for his political hero.

"*Leila Khaled* — the most beautiful terrorist in the world! She was a member of the PFLP — the group that blew up those planes. Have you heard about that? No one died. She's living in Jordan now. She is probably my hero."

I ask what I can get with £2, and he shows me rubber wristbands. I ask if I can get the one with plaits of green, white, and black, and he lets me. A lady who was hiding in the back of the tent asks if I'm a communist. "No, I'm just doing interviews of all the tents." She tells me there is a third communist tent further up the road. I thank her, smiling and bowing my head slightly, and again thank the man who spoke to me, and continue.

At some point during the forty minutes I have spent talking so far, the rain has started. My bag has turned from a bright red to a pessimistic burgundy. I pass the pro-Israeli tent, which is very busy. I consider stopping there, wondering about the moral implications of interviewing these people. I have already heard some interesting views today, and I am sure I will hear more.

*Friends of Israel* is set up exactly halfway down Buchanan Street, where two other prominent thoroughfares meet. This area, due to its proximity to the train station, is very busy. As well as the Zio-tent, there is an evangelist of perhaps 80, wearing a cap, a tucked-in chequered shirt, and a *Die Hard*-like sign saying "JESUS LOVES YOU". There are buskers here too. Sometimes a man with a violin-shaped speaker plays violin recordings of pop songs.

I pass the Israeli tent, and lo and behold: another Palestine tent! A serious-looking person in a blue collar and a hi-vis jacket mans this one alone. His tent is much quieter than all the others, and he looks passionate if not intimidating. He eyeballs my Palestinian-themed wristband, and I hide it from him for the time being.

"Who are you working for, and why do you want to record me?"

The man agrees to be interviewed and I start speaking into my phone. "I am here at the..." The man points to one of his flyers. "...Friends of Palestine Charity Organisation." He stresses his charity ID.

"...We, the PCO, were established in 2015, and before that we were called the Justice for Palestine Centre, established in 2001. As a registered charity organisation, we are helping Palestinians in need, in both the lands of Palestine and internationally... We sell merchandise and collect donations. Our organisation does not take any wages; we are all volunteers.

"...Our aim is to build the best possible home for the Palestinian people — to *remain on their homeland*, not to evacuate. We don't want any more Zionists to migrate to Palestine..."

A young man passes by, shouting at us: "Get a job! Both of you!" We both return to our conversation. He tells me he holds a prestigious academic position in a local institution.

"Do you get a lot of agitation like that? The other Palestine tent told me that the *Friends of Israel* people..."

"Oh, yes..." he interjects. "Just today... I have it recorded on my phone. Just today one of their team started swearing at me and insulting the Prophet Muhammad..." As he says "Muhammad", like some sort of tic, he makes a gesture and says a prayer in his native tongue. "...insulting Islam by saying 'there is no such religion as Islam', and 'Israel for the Israelis.'"

"...I didn't get physical..." He crosses himself again. "But I was thinking of attacking him. I said to them: Israel is not a land; Israel is Jacob..." This amuses me and I let out an uncontrollable, perhaps nervous, laugh. "...Jacob is a prophet of God, son of Abraham. His name is Jacob, and his name is Israel. The people of Israel..." He is now talking about the Twelve Tribes. "...were living in Egypt, and not in Palestine. Abraham is from Iraq, not Palestine! They migrated and lived in peace alongside the Palestinian people..."

He continues. I grow more nervous about where this is going. "...And now you're telling me that Netanyahu, a Pole, and Smotrich — the Israeli Finance Minister — a Ukrainian, and Ariel Sharon, also a Ukrainian, should be here?! Look at Ukraine — what the heck are you doing? Go fight for your own land! Your land is occupied, it's on fire because of *you*, and you're coming to live in Palestine, killing Palestinians, stealing from Palestinians, because the world governments are supporting you, giving you money. America is funding you with all the weapons to kill innocent children..."

I cut him off. "Do you get a lot of support here?", referring to the man who heckled us. "We get a lot of support. I have lived here in Glasgow for 41 years. Here in Glasgow we have Rangers and Celtic, both Christian, both Scottish. *They* want people fighting amongst themselves..."

I return to Ukraine and he uses it to propose his world view to me — that is, his belief in an international Jewish conspiracy.

"My dear friend, let's consider when there used to be the Soviet Union. Two of the Soviet leaders were born in Kyiv, the capital of Ukraine..." He refers here to Brezhnev and Khrushchev. This is not true, but I let him continue. "...and then the Soviet Union fell — who divided it? Who took it? Who is creating all this mess? Zionism!...

"...Zelenskyy has got an Israeli passport. He is an Israeli citizen. *And* he has a great big house in occupied Palestine..." All of these statements are verifiably false, by the way. "...He is an Israeli citizen and he claims to be the President of Ukraine? Let me tell you something else, and this was in the news: there's an area in Ukraine now where only Jewish people are allowed to live, and nobody is to interrupt them, and they're protected by the IDF themselves..."

"...Zionism has infiltrated Ukraine and many other areas. In every country in the world, but most especially in Europe, the governments are controlled by Zionism. That's a fact. The leaders may be replaced but the next one will support it just the same.

"In the Middle East — Libya: who destroyed it? The Israelis and the Americans. The British helped as well. And why? In Libya, at the time of Gaddafi, not one household or person needed help. They were self-sufficient, funded by their own government. They lived like kings. Honestly, they were living luxury lives. Look at Libya now: destroyed after the killing of Gaddafi; all because he wanted independence. Independent currency and gold. America wanted to kick him out. 'He's a terrorist,' they say.

"Now look at Iraq. Did Saddam Hussein ever have any weapons of mass destruction? No —"

"But he did have chemical weapons in the '90s and used them during the Gulf War."

"— And who were attacking them?"

"— The Coalition Forces."

"Exactly, thank you very much. The Coalition Forces, led by America and Israel. And who stole the gold?..."

I don't know as much as I should about the Gulf War.

"Let me tell you... America and Israel. The Americans said 'believe us, if we remove Saddam Hussein, we would have stability in the Middle East' —"

I stop him again. "And do you support Iran?"

He points down to the *Friends of Israel* tent, which is flying, among Stars of David and pride flags, the Iranian flag with a lion holding a sabre in front of a rising sun.

"This is the flag of the Shah of Iran. They were supported by the Israelis. They want to remove the current Iranian regime, calling them terrorists, scum, whatever. They don't know that the Persian culture is the oldest on Earth, and the Iranians have a right to love it."

"Do you not accept that there is popular support for the deposing of the Ayatollah Khomeini?"

"No."

"How can you tell? Nothing is able to come out of Iran."

"Listen, I know the core problem, and I know that the media here tells a lot of lies. I have more knowledge about the situation than anyone here. The media will never tell the truth on the matter. The Iranian regime is not the concern of anyone other than the Iranian people. The Palestinian question is not the concern of anyone other than the Palestinian people. The Iranian people are happy the way they are. The issue is not to do with the current regime. They have the natural resources to be a prosperous nation, and the Americans and Israelis want to use their natural resources for themselves, leaving the Iranians with nothing. That is the issue. It has nothing to do with Islam. Islam is an excuse...

"They want to hide the truth and spread lies so *they* can take control of the land. Since when was the Middle East unstable? Since 1917, when the British Mandate of Palestine was established, and since 1948, when Israel was established, and all the Jewish immigrants from the Second World War migrated to a safe haven. Palestine was fully furnished. We had railways. We had our own aeroplanes. We had passports. In fact, most Israeli leaders came to Palestine with Palestinian passports, and they say we weren't a country?! They sacked all of the Palestinian employees from all the Ministries and replaced them with Jews...

"...The Middle East is the centre of the Earth, and that's why the British wanted to control it. From the river Jordan to the Mediterranean Sea." I am reminded that "Mediterranean" means "middle of the Earth." "...That's why Israel and America want Palestine and Iran now..."

"...Have you stopped recording?"

"Yes, why?"

"Show me." I show him.

"The other Palestine tent keep their money. Look here: I have proof." He opens his phone and shows me videos of hospital operations and communal dinners where people are wearing the logo of his project. "Here, here, here. Anywhere on my photo gallery is the work we have done — I have done — with this stall. The people over there steal the money. They are not a registered charity."

Another young man comes over — the first in ten minutes. He is thin and has a watermelon-themed umbrella. Seeing my "MARXISM FEST" flyer, he asks me if I'm going. I say no, and the young man talks to the stallkeeper. I use this opportunity to slip away.

With this revelation in mind, I decide what I should do. While contemplating, I make my way up to the third socialist stall the lady told me about.

I find a protest outside another subway station. This subway station, still on Buchanan Street, is three-quarters of the way up the road. A semicircle of disabled picketers block the entrance. A sign reads: "ACCESS NOW!" Not under a stand, the Revolutionary Communist Group hold their standard proudly. Young activists are working out which chants to use to fight the tyranny of stairs. They seem busy, and I choose not to interrupt them.

I return to the first pro-Palestine tent.

"I heard from the other tent that you pocket your proceedings?"

"That's a lie, I'm afraid. I used to know him quite well. In fact, I've had him in my house a few times. In truth, he is the person who is pocketing the proceedings. We try and stay true to our cause here and not get involved in any drama."

I feel I've stepped on some toes here. I thank him again for his time and leave before I get completely drenched.
