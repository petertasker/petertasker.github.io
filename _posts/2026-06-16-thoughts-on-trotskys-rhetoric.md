Leon Trotsky (Лев Троцкий; 1879 - 1940) was a Russian revolutionary, historian, and theorist. A key figure in the [Russian Revolution of 1905](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1905) as well as the [October Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/October_Revolution) twelve years later, he lead the Red army from 1918 until 1925 after Lenin's death, at which time Stalin, through a series of political maneuvres, suceeded to the position of premier of the USSR. Trotsky, after being ousted from government by Stalin, spent a few years in Russia in opposition, and was subsequently exiled from Russia, spending a decade in Europe and the Americas. Trotsky would meet his fate at the hands of a Stalinist [NKVD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NKVD) agent, who in August 1940, assassinated the exile in his Mexico City home.

Trotsky would advance on the theories of Marxism-Leninism, forming a sect of socialist thought known as [Trotskyism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trotskyism). Trotsky was critical of [Stalinism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stalinism), and argued, among other things, that the USSR was a "degenerated workers state". I say this not as trivia but to set Trotsky's colours to the mast, and discuss one of my favourite aspects of Trotsky's writing: his polemic style.

While we are setting out stalls, let me declare: I am not a communist. Firstly, I don't believe in labelling myself politically. Secondly, I don't consider myself well-read enough to consider myself a Marxist, Trotskyite, Maoist, Thatcherite--any of it. However, I admire the dedication, the Hegelian idealism, the sense social justice these revolutionaries had, and the will they exhibited by putting their ideas into action--for good and bad. You may call this idolisation of [Great Men](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_man_theory)--fine. I do not undervalue the material factors of the great masses in history. The undercurrents of society were crucial in Trotsky's time--the peasantry, who traded their sickles and sycthes for bayonettes, the factory workers, the women. But consider how long 20th century is chock-full of "Great" men: Bismark, Churchill, Hitler, Lenin, Stalin, Mao... Yes, perhaps history may have unfolded the same way without these leaders at the helm, but perhaps not. Would WW2 have played out the same if it were Chamberlain instead of Churchill? Hoover instead of Roosevelt? Trotsky instead of Stalin?

## In The Balkan Peninsula 
Trotsky, apart from being a revolutionary figure, was a great writer and critic. After being sentenced to exile (you'll start to notice a theme) in 1906, he escaped en-route to his internment to Siberia. He gallivanted around Europe, forming and splitting and forming with multiple left wing socialist and social-democratic parties in Russia and in the more "developed" counties on the continent. He started writing for Pravda (Правда; "Truth") under various nom-de-plumes (e.g., Prince Lvov, A. Oto. Trotsky is in itself a nom-de-guerre; his real name is Bronstein). Aimed at proletarian readers, Pravda was smuggled into Russia and found popularity. 

Trotsky was sent by by Russo-Ukranian paper Kievskaya Mysl (Киевская мысль; "Kyivan Opinion") to report on the Balkan Wars of 1912-13. In this correspondence, now available as a [book](https://www.amazon.co.uk/Balkan-Wars-1912-1913-Leon-Trotsky/dp/0873489071), he interviews Ministers, soldiers, Bulgars, Serbs, wives, and drunks for a completely gripping account of the atrocities on Turks and Albanians, and waste of blood, capital, and time these two wars were. He also, however, displays his marxist feathers and writes scathingly of the political situation in Bulgaria and of the views of his Russian press contemperaries. 

In a manner distinct to the principles of Trotsky, he bemoans the state of press censorship in the military dictatorship of Bulgaria: an absurd situation where reporters are not allowed on the battlefield except pre-approved "slavophile" journalists who (fail to) report the reality on the ground--the killing for the cross and crescent, the rape and murder of peasant workers and women. The mass graves and burning villages of Albanians and Turks for whom the Balkan-allies deem to much of a burden to take as prisoner. One of the first industrialised theatres of destruction, and a pretext to the greatest of wars. 

In order to approve a telegram dispatch, you must pad your report with pro-Bulgar rhetoric, which the Russian reader back home will decode. The censors, instead of laying the onus on the names (or pen-names) on the by-lines of dispatches, lays the onus on itself, completely pointlessly and unforcedly destroying the purpose of correspondence: it is not for Bulgaria to decide what Russian opinion reads and believes, it is Russian opinion. This attitude, one of liberal self-flagellation and Great Power (if we consider the Balkan Federation as an attempt at being a Great Power) meddling, and inability of action, are some of Trotskys biggest bug-bears with the global order of his time. Why must we wait until the war is over to fact check these claims? The blood is being spilt now! 

## Rhetoric
But, here is why Trotsky is so fascinating. At a macro scale, yes, he is a skillful reporter and paints the catastrophe of war and the Balkan War in particular in great quality and details through a Marxist lens, but what I enjoy most about Trotsky is his rhetoric. His name-calling, self-assuredness, and petty ruthlessness.

Trotsky is a historian--he, as well as Lenin, studied the great revolutions in England and France. He was himself a living historical figure! He, in exile, wrote a [history of the Russian Revolution](https://www.marxists.org/archive/trotsky/1930/hrr/), collating not only his own experience but documents and accounts from elsewhere. His historical equipment allows for some excellent rhetorical flourishes on his enemies. He dubs his one of his political opponents, V.V. Toporov, "Toborov XVII", alluding to his stalward and indefensable defence of an Ancien Régime--living in a bubble, refusing to accept the atrocities occuring in the Balkans, being used by liberals to pretend that military nationalism not only acceptale but honourable. 

But history is not the only tool in his belt. Trotsky is a read-up Marxist, which means he comes with fantastic imagery and metaphor to be deployed in myriad ways. He describes the fall of the Tsardom a "Death Agony". Death and agony.
